Illustrative sample. Reynolds & Co is a fictional steel-reinforcement supplier, used to show the exact format and depth of a real Blueprint without exposing a client's numbers. Yours is built entirely from your own Discovery Meeting.

01  Executive Summary

1
Pricing is where the money leaks, not the workshop. Reynolds runs a tight fabrication floor, but every quote is built by hand from the drawings and priced on the estimator's judgement. Two estimators price the same job differently, and nobody tracks which quotes win. Margin is being given away one bid at a time, invisibly.
2
One strategic priority is triggered: margin erosion. On a business turning over £8.5M at a thin reinforcement-supply margin, a few points of pricing drift across the year is the difference between a good year and a worrying one. This overrides normal sequencing.
3
The biggest single-point risk is Dave. The works manager holds the entire production schedule in his head. He is 61 and has talked about winding down. If he goes, on planned notice or not, the floor loses its sequencing brain overnight.
4
The decisive move is visibility, then a funnel. Install the Board Pack so Jane sees margin by job, by month, before the year-end accounts tell her. Add the Sales Module so the bid pipeline stops living in Priya's inbox. A custom estimating assistant is the bigger prize, and it sits outside the standard Modules today.
5
Recommended path. Build the AI Foundation first, the base every Module sits on, then Board Pack, then Sales, with Marketing once the funnel is visible. Two process fixes Jane can start this week cost nothing. Honest verdict: yes, AI helps here, and the highest-value piece is one Aigentic would need to build.

02  Patterns Across the Business

Single-interview engagement (MD only). The founder's view, not a triangulated cross-role synthesis.

Bottlenecks

Critical1. Estimating is slow and inconsistent
Source. Discovery Meeting Q1, Q2, Q4.
Symptoms.
  • Each quote takes two to three days to build by hand from the drawings and the bar bending schedule.
  • Two estimators, two pricing styles. The same job comes out 8% apart depending on who picks it up.
  • Win rate is unknown. Quotes go out, some land, nobody logs which or why.
  • Bigger contractors expect a price in 48 hours. Reynolds sometimes misses the window and the bid dies before it is read.
Root cause. Pricing knowledge lives in two people's heads and a folder of old spreadsheets. There is no rate card, no standard template, and no record that turns won and lost bids into a feedback loop.
Cost of not fixing. Direct margin. On Jane's own estimate, mispriced jobs cost "a few points" across the year. At this turnover, two points is roughly £170K of margin, most of it avoidable.
Fix. Two layers. Short term, a standard quote template and a single rate card so pricing stops depending on who is at the desk (process, this week). Medium term, an AI estimating assistant that reads the schedule and produces a first-pass take-off (a build, see Section 04C).
High2. The schedule lives in one person's head
Source. Discovery Meeting Q3, Q5.
Symptoms.
  • Dave sequences every job on the floor from memory and a whiteboard. No system holds it.
  • Rush jobs get slotted reactively, which pushes planned work and triggers weekend overtime.
  • When Dave is off, sequencing decisions wait for him or get made badly.
Root cause. Twenty-five years of tacit scheduling judgement that was never written down, because for twenty-five years it did not need to be.
Cost of not fixing. Overtime and a concentration risk that turns into a crisis the day Dave retires. He has already raised winding down.
Fix. Not a Module. Document the scheduling logic as a runbook while Dave is still here (process, see Section 04D). A scheduling tool can come later, once the logic is on paper.
High3. Cash is tied up in slow applications and retentions
Applications for payment and retention release are tracked on a spreadsheet that only the bookkeeper fully understands. Retentions get forgotten and chased late, sometimes a year late. Fix: the Board Pack surfaces a live receivables and retention view each month, so nothing falls off the spreadsheet unseen.
Medium4. The pipeline is referral-only
New work comes almost entirely from a handful of repeat contractors. When they go quiet, the floor goes quiet, and there is no inbound to fall back on. Fix: Marketing Module once the funnel is visible, to build a predictable second channel rather than depend on the same five relationships.

Risk

  • Key-person concentration on the works manager. The schedule, supplier relationships and floor judgement run through Dave.
  • Customer concentration. The top three contractors are a large share of revenue. Losing one would hurt.
  • Pricing knowledge undocumented. If an estimator leaves, a chunk of margin judgement leaves with them.

Opportunities

  • Turn estimating from a cost into an edge. Faster, consistent quotes win the 48-hour bids competitors miss.
  • Make margin visible monthly. Seeing job-level margin in week two, not at year-end, changes which work Reynolds chases.

03  Strategic Priorities  —  why act now

One triggered (the hard cap is two). This is an urgency override, the kind of thing mechanical task-scoring would miss.

Priority 1Margin erosion from inconsistent pricing
Trigger: direct profit leak on a thin-margin business. Pricing drift of a couple of points across a year of bids is real money at £8.5M turnover, and it compounds quietly because nobody measures it.
Response: make margin visible by job and by month, and standardise how quotes are priced, before chasing more volume through a leaky funnel.
Expected outcome: Jane sees which jobs make money and which do not, in time to do something about it.
Addressed by: Recommendation B (Board Pack, install first) + Recommendation D (rate card and standard quote template, this week).
Decision behind the priority Reynolds has to decide whether estimating becomes a documented, system-supported function or stays two people's craft. The Board Pack makes the cost of inconsistency visible. The estimating assistant in Section 04C would fix the cause. Doing neither means hoping the next mispriced job is the last one, which it will not be.

04  Recommendation  —  what to do

Does Reynolds & Co need AI?   Yes, and here is where.

The evidence supports a clear recommendation (Verdict A). It starts with the base every Module needs, the AI Foundation, then sequences from there: B (Modules to install, in order), C (the high-value item outside the current Modules), and D (non-AI process changes worth making this week). The mechanical "tasks to automate now" inventory is light here, because the Discovery Meeting was a strategic interview, not a stopwatch walk of the floor.

The base — your AI Foundation

Install this before any Module. Everything below sits on the AI Foundation: a structured layer holding how Reynolds actually works, its numbers, its customers, the way a job flows from enquiry to paid. Without it, an AI Module is guessing at the business. With it, every Module starts already knowing Reynolds.
It earns its place on its own, as a thinking partner Jane can ask anything about the business, before a single Module is added. The Modules below are sequenced on top of it.

B. AI Modules to install on the Foundation

Module 1 · Primary · Install first

Board Pack

Addresses: Strategic Priority 1 (margin erosion) + Bottleneck 3 (cash tied up).

Rationale. Margin is leaking where Jane cannot see it. A monthly pack that shows job-level margin, receivables and retention status turns a year-end surprise into a monthly decision.

Why AI. It pulls the numbers from the accounts and job records on a fixed day, builds the pack, and flags the retentions falling due. What a part-time finance manager assembles in a couple of days, the Module assembles in minutes, and it never forgets a retention.

Cohort pricing: £4K Build + £1.5K/month Optimise. Standard £5K + £2K/month.

Module 2 · Secondary · Once margin is visible

Sales

Addresses: Bottleneck 1 (the funnel side) + Bottleneck 4 (referral-only pipeline).

Rationale. Bids go out and nobody tracks what happens. A pipeline view turns won and lost quotes into a record Reynolds can actually learn from.

Why AI. It logs every bid, tracks win and loss, drafts the follow-up nobody has time to send, and shows where quotes stall. The repetitive chasing that falls through the cracks is exactly what the Module keeps doing without tiring.

Module 3 · Later · When the funnel is visible

Marketing (incl. AEO)

Addresses: Bottleneck 4 (referral-only pipeline).

Rationale. A predictable second channel matters, but only once Reynolds can see and handle the bids it already gets. Build the pipeline before pouring more into the top of it.

Why AI. It plans the content, writes in Reynolds' voice, and tracks how the firm shows up when contractors and specifiers ask AI assistants for reinforcement suppliers. That last piece (AEO) is new ground most competitors are not watching yet.

C. Outside our Modules — the bigger prize

Surfaced honestly. This is a real opportunity where AI would help but no current Aigentic Module fits, which means it is a build, not an install. Recurring items like this are also Aigentic's product radar.

Product radarAI estimating assistant
What it would do. Read the bar bending schedule and drawings, produce a first-pass take-off and a priced quote against a single rate card, and hand the estimator a draft to check rather than a blank sheet.
What it would help with. The headline bottleneck. It attacks both the speed problem (two to three days down to a same-day draft) and the consistency problem (one rate card, not two estimators' habits).
Current status. A custom build, scoped separately from the standard Modules. Flagged as the highest-value piece for Reynolds, and a candidate for a future Aigentic Module.

D. Process changes — non-AI fixes worth making

Things to act on that are not a Module, not AI, and not billable. Some of the fastest moves available.

  1. Write one rate card and one quote template. Why: it removes most of the 8% pricing spread immediately, with no software at all. Effort: a day with both estimators in a room.
  2. Document Dave's scheduling logic while he is still here. Why: the High concentration risk turns into a runbook the floor can follow without him. Effort: a few short sessions, recorded, over a fortnight.
  3. Log every bid, won or lost, in one place. Why: you cannot fix a win rate you have never measured. Even a shared sheet starts the feedback loop the Sales Module later automates. Effort: set up once, two minutes per bid after.

05  Expected Impact

Directional. The Discovery Meeting captured enough to size the pricing leak roughly; the rest is qualitative until the numbers are wired in.

  • Pricing consistency alone, through the rate card, recovers a meaningful slice of the "few points" Jane already suspects she is losing. At two points, that is roughly £170K of margin in play.
  • Monthly margin visibility changes which jobs Reynolds chases, away from busy-but-thin work toward jobs that actually pay.
  • Retentions stop being forgotten, which pulls cash forward that is currently sitting unclaimed.
  • Two process changes (rate card, bid log) cost nothing and can start this week.

Figures are directional and use Reynolds' own estimates where the Discovery Meeting captured them.

Recommended next step

Build the AI Foundation first, the base every Module needs, then install the Board Pack and run it on the next month's numbers, so margin by job becomes visible before the year-end accounts. Write the rate card this week in parallel, it needs no AI at all. A proposal scoping the estimating assistant is the natural follow-on once the Board Pack proves the margin story.
