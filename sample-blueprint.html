The verdict — does AI fit Reynolds & Co?

Yes — but not where you think.

You told us the problem is the pipeline: too much work coming from too few contractors, and not enough of it. Your own answers describe something different. Quotes take two to three days to build when the contractor gives you forty-eight hours. Two estimators price the same job eight per cent apart. Nobody logs which bids land, or why. You are losing work you already have in the door, and giving away margin on the work you win. That is a pricing and conversion problem, not a demand problem, and it is cheaper and faster to fix than finding new customers.

The highest-value thing AI can do for Reynolds this year is get the pricing judgement out of two estimators' heads and into an estimator that drafts the quote — so a price goes back inside the window, and the same job costs the same whoever picks it up. Everything else follows from that.

You already have the enquiries. AI is what stops you losing them, and what stops you underpricing the ones you win.

02  The Next Three Years — AI moves into structural supply

1. Specification moves upstream, into AI, before you are ever called.
Contractors and structural engineers are already using AI assistants to scope reinforcement options and shortlist suppliers before anyone picks up a phone. If the AI doesn't name Reynolds when someone asks who supplies cut-and-bent reinforcement in your region, you are not on the shortlist — and you never find out you were dropped. A buyer arriving pre-briefed by an AI also expects a price back at AI speed. Your forty-eight-hour window is already tight. Within three years it is same-day, and that is the entry ticket rather than an edge.

2. Estimating stops being a craft and becomes a system.
The judgement in an experienced estimator's head — how a bar bending schedule really prices, what steel movement does to a quote signed today and delivered in six weeks, which tenders are quietly unprofitable — is exactly what AI is now good at encoding. The firms that come through this intact are the ones that captured it before the estimator walked. You have two people holding that judgement and an eight per cent spread between them, which tells you it was never written down. Your larger competitors have the scale to absorb a departure. Your edge is responsiveness, and responsiveness is precisely what an encoded estimator protects.

3. Take-off and detailing collapse toward zero marginal cost.
Reading a drawing and a bar bending schedule to produce a first-pass take-off is about to get radically cheaper. That changes the economics of bidding: when a take-off costs minutes rather than two days of a scarce estimator, you can price every serious enquiry and still return it inside the window. It also changes which work is worth chasing, because the jobs you currently decline on capacity grounds stop being expensive to quote.

Where you fit Where Reynolds sits inside each of these shifts — and the specific moves that follow — is exactly what the AI Foundation maps in detail.

03  What We Found

Drawn from your Discovery Meeting. Single-interview engagement (MD only) — the founder's view, not a triangulated cross-role synthesis. Where a figure is inferred rather than something you told us, we say so.

Where work gets stuck

Critical1. Quote speed and pricing consistency
What's happening. Every quote is built by hand from the drawings and the bar bending schedule, and it takes two to three days. The bigger contractors expect a price in forty-eight hours, so some bids die before they are read. Worse, the two estimators price the same job about eight per cent apart, because pricing depends on who happens to be at the desk.
Root cause. Pricing knowledge lives in two people's heads and a folder of old spreadsheets. No rate card, no standard template, and no record that turns won and lost bids into a feedback loop.
Cost of leaving it. Two ways at once. You lose winnable work you already paid to acquire, which is the most expensive kind of loss there is. And you underprice some of what you win, invisibly, one bid at a time.
Critical2. No bid follow-up — and no record of why you lose
What's happening. Bids go out and live in Priya's inbox. Nobody owns the chase, and no outcome is recorded. Reynolds cannot say why it lost any job, or who won it.
Root cause. No owner, no cadence, no system of record. The win rate is not a figure anyone can produce.
Cost of leaving it. Beyond the bids that die of neglect: you are about to make large decisions blind. Whether the pipeline is really the problem, whether the premium holds, which contractors are worth the effort — every one of those is answerable from a win/loss log, and unanswerable without it.
High3. The schedule lives in one person's head
Dave sequences every job on the floor from memory and a whiteboard. Rush jobs get slotted reactively, which pushes planned work and triggers weekend overtime. When he is off, sequencing waits for him or gets made badly. Twenty-five years of tacit judgement that was never written down, because for twenty-five years it did not need to be. He is sixty-one and has raised winding down.
High4. Cash is tied up in slow applications and retentions
Applications for payment and retention release are tracked on a spreadsheet only the bookkeeper fully understands. Retentions get forgotten and chased late, sometimes a year late. That is your own money, sitting unclaimed, on a business where a couple of margin points matter.
Medium5. The pipeline is referral-only
New work comes almost entirely from a handful of repeat contractors. When they go quiet, the floor goes quiet, and there is no inbound to fall back on. Worth fixing — but after you can see and handle the bids you already get, not before.

Repetitive Load

Your enquiry-to-despatch chain is full of repetitive, rules-based work: screening enquiries, rekeying schedules, chasing drawings, logging bids, raising applications, chasing retentions. Every one is a candidate for removal. But we can't size it yet. The Discovery Meeting was a strategic interview, not a stopwatch walk of the floor, so nobody asked "roughly how many hours a week does that eat?" — and we won't invent the number. Half an hour on the floor fixes this permanently.

Risk

  • Key-person concentration on Dave. The schedule, the supplier relationships and the floor judgement all run through one man who has raised retirement. No date has been confirmed.
  • Decisions made blind. No win/loss record exists, so the pipeline question you most want answered cannot currently be answered.
  • Pricing knowledge undocumented. If either estimator leaves, a chunk of margin judgement leaves with them — and the eight per cent spread says nobody could reconstruct it.
  • Customer concentration. The top three contractors are a large share of revenue. Losing one would hurt more than the pipeline problem you named.

Opportunities

  • The forty-eight-hour window is winnable. Competitors miss it too. Same-day pricing turns your slowest step into the reason you get the call.
  • One rate card removes most of the spread immediately. A decision, not a build.
  • Monthly margin visibility changes which work you chase — away from busy-but-thin, toward jobs that actually pay.
  • Retentions are cash you have already earned. Claiming them on time needs a list and a date, not a strategy.

04  Why Act Now

One priority passed the test. We looked hard for a second and did not find one that met the bar — padding this section would waste your attention.

Priority 1Margin erosion from inconsistent pricing
The trigger: a direct profit leak on a thin-margin business. Pricing drift of a couple of points across a year of bids is real money at £8.5M of turnover, and it compounds quietly because nothing measures it. You told us you suspect you are losing "a few points". Two points is roughly £170K.
Why scoring alone would miss it. Mechanical scoring ranks tasks by hours and repeatability. Estimating looks unremarkable on that basis: two people, a few days a week, nothing alarming. It cannot see that the same task is silently setting the price of every job in the business.
Cadence. The rate card this week, because it costs a day and removes most of the spread on its own. The estimator build starts alongside it, and the monthly margin view lands before the next quarter closes.
The decision behind it. Reynolds has to decide whether estimating becomes a documented, system-supported function, or stays two people's craft. Doing neither means hoping the next mispriced job is the last one, which it will not be.
From the operator — 20 years running an SME

I have watched pricing drift eat a business from the inside, and it never announces itself. In the company I co-founded, we let each salesperson price their own deals. Everyone was experienced, everyone was trusted, and every one of them was quietly a little different. No single deal looked wrong. It took us two years to notice that our average price had walked several points downhill, because nobody was comparing like with like — there was nothing to compare against. The damage isn't the one bad quote. It's that without a rate card you cannot even see the pattern, so it runs for years before anyone names it. Reynolds has two estimators eight per cent apart on the same job. You already have the evidence I didn't have. That is worth more than it sounds. Use it.

05  What To Do

Verdict: YES — and the fastest return is on work you already have.

Four things, in the order they matter to Reynolds. The first is the one that sets the price of everything else.

The base — your AI Foundation

The AI Foundation is simply the business, written down in a form AI can use — your strategy, your numbers, your customers, how a job actually flows from enquiry to paid. Without it, every AI answer is generic. With it, the answers are yours. Everything below sits on top of it, and it earns its place on its own as a thinking partner you can ask anything about the business, before a single thing is installed. (~30 days, kickoff to handover.)

A. Get the pricing out of two heads and into an estimator

Start now · the biggest single move available

What it is. Schedule in, priced quote out, same day. Two layers: a calculator that reads the bar bending schedule and produces the take-off (rules and arithmetic), and a quote engine that holds the pricing judgement your estimators currently carry in their heads, made explicit and applied the same way every time.

Why it's first. It solves both of your problems with one build. It closes the forty-eight-hour gap that is losing you winnable bids, and it ends the eight per cent spread that is quietly setting your margin. Every other recommendation here measures or reports the problem. This one removes the cause.

What we don't know yet. Whether the quote engine can be fully automated, or only assisted. Nobody has ever written down how your estimators build a price — the eight per cent spread is the proof. That is why the first month is capture and the second is build. We don't start building before we know what it has to do. This is a genuine bet, and we would rather say so now than discover it in month three.

B. See the margin while you can still act on it

Monthly P&L, job margin and cash — an AI finance director

What it is. Margin by job and by month, the drivers underneath the numbers, a pack built automatically on a fixed date, and a live view of what you're owed — including which retentions fall due.

What it fixes. You currently learn which jobs made money when the year-end accounts arrive, which is far too late to do anything about it. It also stops retentions ageing quietly on a spreadsheet only one person understands.

Why AI does this better than a person. The pack isn't late through incompetence — assembling it is a manual job that always loses to whatever is on fire. AI produces it on the same date every month whether anyone chases it or not, and tracks the drivers, so a job running thin surfaces while you can still act on it rather than after year-end when you can only regret it. It is not cleverer than your bookkeeper. It is simply never too busy.

C. Put a system behind the bid

Pipeline, follow-up and win/loss — an AI sales manager

What it is. It tracks the pipeline, chases every bid that goes out, and records the outcome of each one: won, lost, why, and to whom.

What it fixes. Bids currently live in an inbox and die of neglect. It also gives you, for the first time, a reason on every lost job — the evidence base for the pipeline question you actually want answered.

Why AI does this better than a person. Bid follow-up fails in every small business for the same reason: it's nobody's favourite job, it's invisible when skipped, and there is always something more urgent. Someone promises to chase, then a live job blows up. AI reads every bid as it's issued, chases on a fixed cadence no matter how busy the week is, and captures the reason in the customer's own words. It never feels awkward about the third follow-up. It is simply incapable of forgetting.

D. Get found when buyers ask an AI

Content and AI visibility — an AI marketing manager · starts quietly now

What it is. Plans and writes content in your voice on a schedule, and runs AEO (Answer Engine Optimisation): making sure Reynolds is named when a contractor asks an AI assistant who supplies cut-and-bent reinforcement in your region. The new version of showing up first on Google, except the buyer now asks an AI and acts on the two or three names it returns.

The reframe. You called this the main problem — not enough people know about us. But your best work comes from four or five contractor relationships you can name. That's not a brand-awareness problem, it's a targeting problem, and a much easier one.

Why not first. AI visibility takes months to move, so the baseline starts now. But it does not fix this quarter, and it shouldn't take your attention while your pricing is eight per cent apart from itself.

E. Changes worth making that need no AI at all

Fast, free, and yours to act on this month
  1. Write one rate card and one quote template. It removes most of the eight per cent spread immediately, with no software at all, and it is the thing the estimator gets built from. A day with both estimators in a room.
  2. Name the person who logs every bid, won or lost. Every result recorded: won, lost, why, who got it. The cheapest data Reynolds will ever collect, and the evidence base for everything else here. It needs a name against it or it won't happen. One decision, then two minutes per bid.
  3. Get Dave's scheduling logic on paper while he is still here. A few recorded sessions turn twenty-five years of judgement into a runbook the floor can follow. A few short sessions over a fortnight.
  4. Confirm Dave's actual intentions. He has raised winding down; nobody has asked him for a date. Everything about the floor's next two years depends on the answer. One conversation.
  5. List the open retentions and put a date against each. That is your own cash, already earned. Half a day, once.

Parked, not forgotten: a scheduling tool for the floor is a real opportunity, but it can only be built once Dave's logic is on paper. Do the runbook first (E3). The tool is the natural follow-on, not the starting point.

06  Expected Impact

This section would normally put a number of hours a week against each recommendation. We can't do that yet, so we haven't.

  • Why not. The Discovery Meeting was a strategic interview, not a stopwatch walk of the floor. You gave us per-job timings — two to three days to build a quote — but not how many quotes a week, so any hours-per-week figure here would be invented. Half an hour on the floor fixes it permanently.
  • What we can say, from your own numbers. At £8.5M of turnover, the couple of points you suspect you're losing is roughly £170K a year. Most of it is avoidable, and the rate card alone — which costs you a day and nothing else — recovers a meaningful slice of it.
  • Quote turnaround. Directionally the highest-leverage change available, because it acts on demand you have already won and paid to acquire.
  • The estimator. Ends the dependence on two people's habits and makes a same-day quote the default rather than an aspiration.
  • Retentions. Cash you have already earned, currently ageing on a spreadsheet. Claiming it on time is a list and a date, not a project.

Figures are directional and use Reynolds' own estimates where the Discovery Meeting captured them. Where hours could not be captured, we have said so rather than estimated.

Recommended next step

Three things, in this order. One: write the rate card — a day with both estimators, no AI, and it removes most of the spread on its own. Two: give us half an hour on the floor about volumes, so the next version of this document puts real numbers where the gaps currently are. Three: name the person who logs every bid from this week, because it costs nothing and it is the foundation of every decision above. Then the Foundation and the estimator build run on our side.
